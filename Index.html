  • To JoeBeau, or Short, or JoeBi Wan. My Tommy Wiseau - for everything. For Walking Softly. Miss you, brother (If you see this, don’t laugh!).

    On the day of the funeral, your mom said you called her. She said that there was the most perfect pink cloud in the dawn sky, and she knew it was you. This beautiful boy, bright and buoyant, wanted to watch the sunrise with her. I went and visited you in your plot in Richmond not too long ago. I was headed to Cole and Henry’s and made a detour. Met with your mom, and sister Hannah. I don’t know where the rest of your family was, but we talked and cried and laughed. Nobody had answers still, and that had not settled in.

    • The Last Bear Hunt

  • I don’t remember what the sky looked like. I don’t remember who I was then, or what I looked like, but I remember being barefoot. I remember how soft the uncut grass felt after walking through our gravel driveway. I remember it being warm enough to feel relieved by the breeze. I was alone, looking through the bottom board of the border fence, my gaze out toward an empty field in Round Hill, Virginia. I heard a door shut in the distance behind me, and I saw my father moving to meet me. The edge of the fence ran rough against my skin as I climbed up to the top board. I wanted to be right there next to him, staring out at the fields and woods that surrounded us. I wanted to be at his level.

    • Healing and Hearing, Sadness and Sound

  • Introduction - Swims

    When I was twelve, I rediscovered my earliest memory of my dad. It was of him during the summer time. I remember being small, and feeling a gravel path beneath me hurting my feet, and looking up at him with eyes squinted by the brightness of the sun. We were headed toward the pool in our backyard. He was looking down toward me and smiling. His smile was as big as he was to me. He bent down to hug me and I remember giggling when his scratchy beared touch my cheek. The next thing I remember is the feeling of the freezing water after he’d thrown me in, and how big of a splash he’d made diving in after me. I remember how it sounded, not the splashing of the water, or his voice, or the birds, or anyone elses voice, but the song coming from the open kitchen window. It was quiet, but I heard it clear and felt its rhythm warm the icy water around me. It was an acoustic guitar and a harmonica playing in tandem and it made the people outside of the pool sing “come on, come on down”.
